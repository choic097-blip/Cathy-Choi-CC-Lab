A Daily Diagnostic for Creators — Today's Creative State

01. Concept Proposal

Community — Creators. Specifically, self-directed creative practitioners (illustrators, writers, game designers, student designers) who work across multiple creative modes and rarely have external structure telling them what to work on today. This is a community I belong to directly.

The Challenge — Talented creators tend to have too many interesting options. The problem isn't a shortage of ideas; it's an imbalance between Input and Output. Wanting to watch a comic is not the same as wanting to draw one. Wanting to absorb a worldbuilding reference is not the same as wanting to build one. Most creative-productivity tools treat creators as uniformly "productive" or "resting." They don't account for the fact that different mental states demand genuinely different activities.

The Solution — A 9-question daily diagnostic that maps the user onto one of four existential types: ARC (The Archive — drained, needs input), LOG (The Logic Gate — data overload, needs structuring), CAT (The Catalyst — pressure peak, needs release), RUN (The Runtime — prepped, needs to ship). Each option contributes a point to one of the four types; the highest-scoring type is the user's state for the day. The result page shows their type, a score distribution, and a curated list of activities. A secondary analysis page dives deeper into each type's diagnosis.

Why a Quiz — A low-friction checkpoint. A few minutes of structured reflection with no tracking, no logins, no moralizing. It reflects the creator's state back to them in a legible form and proposes a small next step — reframing their quiet hours not as "rest vs work" but as distinct existential states with their own optimal activities.

Stack — HTML, CSS, Vanilla JavaScript. No libraries. Data persists in localStorage. Four pages: landing → quiz → result → analysis.

References — Julia Cameron (The Artist's Way) on the input/output creative cycle, Cal Newport (Deep Work) on states of focus, Are.na's framing of archival-as-creation, MBTI / Enneagram as cautionary models — studying what they get right (the satisfaction of a named state) and what to avoid (treating a dynamic state as fixed identity).
